Where Water Wanders

Water on Earth is incredibly old. The water in your glass today may have passed through a volcano... a glacier... or even a dinosaur.

So how is that possible?

Because water is always on the move. It travels through a continuous journey called the water cycle — powered by energy from the Sun.

In this story, you’ll explore how water moves through the world, and you’ll build a model of the water cycle along the way.

Goin’ Up!

Water doesn't just stay in oceans, lakes, or puddles. It moves upward — into the air — in a process called evaporation.

When the Sun shines, it gives energy to water molecules on the surface. Some of those molecules get just enough energy to break free and float away as invisible water vapor.

Did you know? Evaporation isn't the same as boiling. Boiling happens when all the water gets hot and bubbles. Evaporation only happens at the surface — even in a cold puddle.

Plants Join In: Transpiration

But water doesn’t just leave from lakes and oceans. It also escapes from plants!

Plants take up water from their roots and release it through tiny openings in their leaves. As the Sun warms the leaves, water evaporates into the air — just like from a puddle. This process is called transpiration.

Fun fact: A single large tree can release over 100 gallons of water vapor in a day.

What Goes Up, Must Come Down

Imagine if water vapor just kept going up forever. Luckily, Earth's atmosphere has a plan.

As water vapor rises, it cools down. Cooler vapor molecules slow down, bump into each other, and stick together. This forms tiny liquid droplets — a process called condensation.

These droplets gather around tiny particles in the air (like dust or smoke) to form clouds.

[Animation placeholder: cloud formation from droplets]

Time to Fall: Precipitation

Clouds don’t hold water forever.

Inside a cloud, tiny droplets crash into each other and combine. Over time, they grow heavier and heavier — until gravity pulls them back down to Earth.

This falling water is called precipitation.

It can fall as rain, snow, sleet, or hail, depending on the temperature and altitude.

Sometimes, water vapor skips the liquid stage and turns directly into ice — this is called crystallization, and it forms snowflakes high in the sky.

[Animation placeholder: raindrop formation and falling]

All Together Now

The water cycle is a model scientists use to explain how water moves through Earth’s systems.

  • Evaporation from water surfaces
  • Transpiration from plants
  • Condensation to form clouds
  • Precipitation as rain or snow
  • Crystallization to form ice

Together, these steps show how water changes states, moves through systems, and is powered by the Sun’s energy.

Beyond the Cycle: Fresh Water Matters

Water on Earth is constantly recycled — but fresh water is limited.

Only about 3% of Earth’s water is fresh, and most of that is frozen in glaciers. The water we use for drinking, farming, and bathing must be carefully conserved.

Think about this: How does the water cycle connect to climate change? What happens to the cycle when the planet warms?
